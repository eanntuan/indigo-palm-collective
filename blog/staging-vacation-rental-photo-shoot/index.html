Five Days Before the Photo Shoot: What It Actually Takes to Stage a Vacation Rental

By Eann Tuan · May 4, 2026 · 7 min read

The Casa Moto photo shoot is May 14. Today is May 4. That sounds like ten days of comfortable lead time until you sit down and map out what still needs to happen and realize that the actual install window is five days: May 9 to 13. Two days of those are for furniture delivery. One day is for art, plants, and the details that take four times longer than you think. One day is pure contingency. The last day is clean and reset.

This post is a real-time account of how we're running the prep, not a polished retrospective. The shoot is with Dawn Asher, who designed the property, and the photographer she uses. The stakes are that these photos will be the listing photos for the foreseeable future, so getting the staging wrong has a long tail.

Here's what five days of vacation rental staging actually looks like from the inside.

Day one of install week. Before anything is pretty, everything is boxes. This is the part people don't post on Instagram.

Why Vacation Rental Photography Staging Is Different

Staging a home for sale and staging a vacation rental for photography are not the same thing. Home staging is about removing personality and letting buyers project themselves onto blank surfaces. Vacation rental photography is almost the opposite: you want specific, strong personality that communicates an experience before someone books.

A buyer looking at a listing doesn't need to picture themselves eating on your back patio. A guest does. Every photo should make someone think: that's exactly where I would sit with coffee. That's exactly the pool I want to swim in on a Thursday afternoon. The photos aren't documentation. They're a promise.

That changes what you stage, how you stage it, and what you deliberately leave out. A vacation rental photo that looks like a catalog showroom has the same problem as a dating profile photo where no one is smiling.

Days 9-10 Before the Shoot: What Gets Decided Now

The work that happens before install week is mostly decisions. What goes in each room, how each shot should read, what's missing that would make a photo better versus worse. Dawn and I walk through this on a call about a week before delivery day.

The questions we work through: Is there anything in the space right now that reads poorly on camera? (Bare walls photograph badly. Rooms without plants look flat. Outdoor spaces without shade anchors look inhospitable.) What's the sequence of rooms we want to shoot? Where does natural light hit each room and at what time? Is there anything on the current shopping list that won't arrive in time?

For Casa Moto, the main pre-shoot gaps are: the primary bedroom still needs its canopy hung, the kitchen nook credenza hasn't arrived yet, and the patio needs its outdoor projector mounted to the pergola. Those are the three load-bearing items. Everything else is styling.

Day 5: The Delivery Gauntlet

The Casa Moto renovation involved more delivery coordination than I expected when I started this process. The Antigua rocking chairs come from one vendor. The pool loungers from another. The fire pit coffee table from Neighbor. The kitchen nook credenza from a third place. None of these coordinate with each other. They all show up on different days with different four-hour delivery windows.

Install day one is logistics management. The goal is to get everything physically inside the property and in the right room so that day two can be actual staging rather than moving heavy furniture up the hallway.

The thing I've learned: label everything before it goes in. Tape a note on each box with what room it's going to. It sounds obvious. In the chaos of three delivery trucks showing up within two hours of each other, it prevents the sofa from ending up in the primary bedroom and the bedroom armoire in the living room.

Install day. The goal is to get everything in the right room. Styling comes later. First you just need things to be in the correct space.

Day 4: Unboxing and Placement

Day four is the day that feels like the least progress and takes the longest. Everything is out of boxes, placed in rooms, and looks slightly worse than when it arrived because nothing is positioned correctly yet.

Furniture placement for photography is not the same as furniture placement for living. For photos, you optimize for the camera angle, not for the flow of the room. The sofa might sit a foot further from the wall than you would put it for actual use because it photographs better with a little air behind it. The bed needs space on both sides for symmetrical shots even if one side is against the wall in reality.

The other thing that happens on day four: you discover what's still missing. There's always something. For us, it's usually a small table that ties two seating pieces together, or a plant that fills a corner that reads as dead space from camera height. I keep a running list during unboxing. Whatever's on that list goes on an emergency order or a same-day pickup run.

Day 3: Styling Starts in Earnest

Styling is where the property starts to look like itself rather than a furniture showroom. It's also the part that takes far more time than first-time stagers budget for, including me on my first property.

The ratio that holds in my experience: 70% of styling time is spent on 30% of the space. The spots that need the most attention are usually the bedside table setups, the kitchen counter moments, and whatever the hero outdoor shot will be. Those three areas probably get four to six hours combined. Everything else gets the remaining time.

At Casa Moto, the patio is the hero shot. The Antigua rocking chairs need to be angled toward each other in a way that reads as invitation rather than furniture storage. The umbrella needs to be positioned to catch the right light. The pool loungers need towels, and the towels need to be the exact right colors (terracotta and ivory, not white, not gray). The fire pit coffee table needs something on it: a tray, a candle, a book that looks deliberately left there rather than randomly placed.

Pillow staging. You go through three configurations before you land on the one that photographs correctly. The pile on the floor is normal.

What Makes a Vacation Rental Photo Actually Work

There's a specific thing that separates a good vacation rental photo from a bad one, and it's not the furniture or the photographer. It's whether the space looks like someone was just there.

A perfectly symmetrical room with matching accessories and no signs of life looks like a showroom. A room that has a book left face-down on the side table, a partially empty glass on the coffee table, a throw at the foot of the bed that looks sat on rather than draped, that looks like a place a person actually wants to be.

Dawn calls this "lived-in warmth." It's the opposite of sterile. Achieving it deliberately is harder than it sounds because the instinct when staging is to make everything perfect, which is exactly wrong. You want 95% perfect and 5% human.

The specific items that create this effect in photos, in our experience: a tray with a few things on it rather than bare surfaces, plants that have actual fullness rather than stick-thin dried arrangements, outdoor furniture that has position rather than lineup, and lighting that looks like it was turned on because someone is using the space rather than for the camera.

Day 2: The Details That Photograph

Day two of styling is detail day. The big pieces are placed. Now you're working on what the camera actually sees up close: the tray vignette on the primary nightstand, the cookbook on the kitchen counter propped at an angle, the succulent arrangement on the outdoor table, the towels folded in the bathroom exactly right.

Details that look fine in person often read as flat on camera. The opposite is also true: small objects that you'd never notice walking through a room can anchor a photo and make it feel complete. The discipline here is photographing each room as you finish it. Not with a professional camera, just your phone. This shows you what the camera sees and reveals the gaps that your eyes in the room don't catch.

At Casa Moto, the Wild Tropics bedroom is going to be one of the hero shots. That room photographs dramatically: floor-to-ceiling tropical wallpaper, Merola tile headboard, terracotta pendant lamps. The styling for it is relatively minimal because the room itself is doing the work. What it needs is a book on the nightstand, a throw at the foot of the bed, and the pendant lamps at the right height. Three things. But each of those three things has to be exactly right.

Mid-staging. It looks like chaos. By end of day it reads as intentional. This is normal progress.

Day 1: Clean, Reset, Contingency

The day before the shoot is intentionally left open. In practice it fills up with every small thing that wasn't quite right from the styling days, plus whatever emergency detail run reveals itself at 8am.

The mandatory items: clean the entire property. Not guest-ready clean. Photo-ready clean. That means fingerprints off every glass surface, pool cleaned and balanced so the water reads clearly, outdoor furniture wiped down, any water spots on tile dealt with. The camera is unforgiving about things that read as maintenance issues rather than design choices.

It also means a final walk-through with the shot list from the planning call. Is every room where it needs to be? Is there anything in the property that doesn't belong in the photo (extension cords, cleaning supplies under the sink that might be in frame, a throw pillow that arrived in the wrong colorway)? The final walk-through catches the things you stopped seeing because you've been in the space for five days.

The Shoot Itself

The shoot is May 14. By then, the property will have been through four months of renovation, a full install week, and more deliberate attention than most rentals get in years. The photos will show a version of Terra Luz that's staged for the camera but should also accurately represent what guests experience when they walk in.

The bar for vacation rental photography isn't perfection. It's accuracy plus aspiration. The photos should make someone want to book, and then when they arrive, the reality should match or exceed what they were expecting. If the photos are better than the actual property, you get disappointed guests. If the photos undersell the actual property, you lose bookings to listings that oversell theirs.

Getting that calibration right is what the five-day prep is actually for.
