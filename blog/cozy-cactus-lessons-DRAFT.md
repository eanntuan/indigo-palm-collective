Three years ago I handed keys to my first guests, crossed every finger I had, and refreshed my phone for 72 hours straight. I had no idea what I was doing. I had a newly renovated house, a label maker, and a lot of confidence that I'd figured most of it out.

I had not figured most of it out.

Here's what three years actually taught me. 191 reviews, four properties, and at least two incidents I'd rather forget.

---

## Things Break at the Worst Possible Time

Not sometimes. Always.

The week before Coachella is when things go wrong. That's the rule. I didn't make it up, the universe did, and I've stopped fighting it.

Tile grout cracking? Coachella week. Hot tub thermometer acting up? Coachella weekend. The week a dishwasher decided to retire? You know when.

What I've learned: have a list. An actual list of local contractors who pick up the phone and can show up same-day. Not a Yelp tab you'll Google at 8pm on a Friday when a guest messages you. A list. Names. Numbers. The plumber who texted back in 20 minutes when water was pooling under the sink. The handyman who drove out on a Sunday. You need these people before you need them.

I keep a saved contact called "HVAC EMERGENCY" in my phone. Michael, the electrician-plumber combo who has bailed me out multiple times, is in there as "MICHAEL CAGLE - CALL FIRST." He knows my properties. He's been there. That relationship took a year to build and it's worth more than any appliance warranty.

## What a 1-Star Review Actually Teaches You

You will get one. If you're hosting long enough, you will get a review that makes you want to close the listing entirely and move to a cabin with no wifi.

Mine came in year two. I won't share the specifics because this isn't a grudge post. But what I'll say is: I read it six times looking for something to argue with, and there was one thing I couldn't argue with. One detail that was fair. Something I'd overlooked because I'd stopped seeing the house with fresh eyes.

That's the thing about a bad review. If you can get past the anger long enough to actually read it, there's usually one sentence that's right. And that sentence is worth more than a hundred five-star reviews because it shows you something you missed.

I fixed the thing. Then I fixed two other things I noticed while I was at it. I wrote back, calmly. And then I moved on.

What I didn't do: argue publicly. Offer a refund in exchange for removing the review. Or pretend it didn't happen. None of those help you.

The review is public. Your response is also public. Future guests are reading both. The response where you're defensive and accusatory is going to lose you more bookings than the original complaint.

## What Repeat Guests Are Actually Telling You

I have guests who've come back three, four times. Coachella every year. Families who stayed before they had kids and came back with a stroller and two car seats, specifically because they trusted the setup would be there.

Repeat guests are the metric that matters. Anyone can get a first booking. Getting someone to come back is harder.

What they're telling you when they return: you didn't disappoint. That's the whole thing. They came with expectations set by the last visit. They're betting their vacation on you. That's a different kind of trust than a first-time guest who doesn't know what they're walking into.

A guest who returns is also, quietly, doing your marketing for you. They've told people. They've recommended you. They've defended you in group chats when someone suggested a different rental. You just don't see that part.

## Systems Beat Vibes Every Single Time

In year one I was running on vibes. I'd show up between guests, assess the situation, make a list in my Notes app, go buy whatever seemed to be missing. It worked, sort of. It was also exhausting and inconsistent.

What I have now: a staging checklist. Every room. Every drawer. Every cabinet. Not because I'm type-A (okay, a little type-A), but because the checklist is what makes the house the same every time regardless of who's cleaning it, what happened with the last guests, or how tired I am.

The label maker wasn't just for guests. It was for the checklist. "Baby spoons: left drawer, blue label." "Extra lightbulbs: hall closet, top shelf." "Pool float pump: red bin in garage." When your cleaner doesn't have to guess, things end up where they belong. When things are where they belong, guests don't message you at 9pm asking where they are.

I had a guest tell me she walked through the house on arrival just to look at the labels. She said it felt like someone had actually thought about the visit before she arrived. That's exactly what it is.

## The Thing You Can't Outsource

You can hire a cleaner. You can hire a handyman. You can set up automated messages, keypad codes, smart locks. There's a version of this business where you're mostly hands-off, and it works fine.

What you can't automate is the part where a guest is panicking and needs a human on the other end of the phone.

The 11pm message when the baby won't sleep and they can't find the sound machine. (It's in the closet. Labeled. But 11pm panic is a different brain state.) The question about whether the community pool is heated. The family who locked themselves out and has a sleeping toddler in the car.

Those moments are the ones guests remember. Not the throw pillows. Not the coffee bar. The moment someone responded in two minutes at 11pm and helped them figure it out.

I respond under a minute. Have since day one. Not because I have no life (I do, mostly), but because when you're a guest and something goes wrong, the wait feels like an hour. Two minutes isn't slow. Two minutes is someone who is paying attention.

## What I'd Do Differently

Go slower on the décor. Go faster on the systems.

I spent months on furniture decisions in year one. The right rug. The right throw pillow. Does this work with this? I agonized over a gallery wall for three weeks. Meanwhile, the staging checklist I actually needed took me about four hours to build once I finally sat down and did it.

The photos are what gets someone to book. The systems are what gets them to come back. Both matter. But they're not equally hard to build, and I had my priorities backwards for the first year.

I also wish someone had told me: your first version of the house is not your final version. Things you think are permanent, like that rug you drove to Culver City to pick up, get replaced. Styles evolve. Guests leave evidence of what's working and what isn't. The house you have in year three looks nothing like what you launched with, and that's a good sign.
