# Eann Tuan — Writing Voice Guide
## For blog posts, captions, emails, copy, and any Indigo Palm Collective content

---

## The one-line version

Write like you're telling a friend what actually happened. Specific, honest, a little self-deprecating. No performance.

---

## What your voice actually sounds like

**Short sentences. Fragments are fine.**
"Painted the walls white, all of them. Ripped out the carpet. Got rid of the boob lights."

**Parentheticals for asides and self-awareness.**
"(My own home is not this organized. My guests live better than I do.)"
"(aka me)"
"(Accurate.)"

**Specific locations and details, not vague gestures.**
Not: "I found a foosball table online."
Yes: "Picked it up in Hermosa Beach. Somehow got it in and out of my car."

**Name the specific thing. Always.**
Not: "I bought a good high chair."
Yes: "I bought a Stokke high chair instead of the wobbly plastic disaster from the clearance aisle."

Not: "furniture store in LA."
Yes: "Interiors Made Eezy in Culver City."

**Self-deprecating but not self-pitying.**
"Like a very enthusiastic person had been set loose in a home goods store with a credit card and no plan. (Accurate.)"
"Some paintings by a local artist (aka me) that I attempted to place around the home. None of them made the cut."

**Natural speech patterns. Don't clean it up too much.**
"it ain't for the faint of heart"
"I am here to tell you that friendship was tested that day"
"Annoyingly, completely, repeatedly right."

**Dry humor, not jokes.**
"The boob lights had to go." (just state the fact, don't over-explain it)
"Matching Tupperware lids were unprecedented." (let the absurdity land on its own)

**Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph.**
If a paragraph is more than 4 sentences, break it.

**No em dashes.** Use periods or commas instead.

**No hollow openers.** Never start with: "In today's world," "As a host," "When it comes to," "I'm excited to share."

---

## Sentence patterns that sound like you

- Statement. Short follow-up. Punchline or admission.
- "Not [generic version]. [Specific true version]."
- "(Parenthetical that admits something honest.)"
- Lists that build: "Garage sales. Marshalls. TJ Maxx. HomeGoods. Ross."
- Rhetorical question you immediately answer: "Which one goes here? Do these colors work? I had no idea."

---

## What to avoid

- "Luxury" / "curated" / "world-class" / "elevated experience"
- Em dashes
- Hollow transitions: "That said," "At the end of the day," "It goes without saying"
- Adjective stacking: "beautifully designed, thoughtfully curated, carefully selected"
- Overly tidy conclusions that wrap everything up with a bow
- Explaining the joke

---

## Tone by content type

**Blog posts:** First-person, story-driven, specific. Reads like a smart person recounting something that actually happened.

**Instagram captions:** Punchy opener (the thing that makes you stop scrolling), 2-3 short lines of context, optional soft CTA. No hashtag dumps.

**Email to guests:** Warm, direct, specific to their stay. Sign off as Eann, not "The Indigo Palm Team."

**Property descriptions:** Sensory and specific. "Hot tub under desert stars" not "relaxing outdoor amenities."

**Host tips / blog:** Honest about what went wrong and what you learned. The self-deprecation is the credibility.
