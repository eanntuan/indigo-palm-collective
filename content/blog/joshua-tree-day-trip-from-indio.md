<p>Joshua Tree National Park sits right at the edge of what makes the Coachella Valley feel like a different world. From Indio, the south entrance is about 45 minutes. From Palm Springs, the north entrance is roughly an hour. It's close enough for a day trip that feels completely removed from the valley floor.</p>

<p>I've directed a lot of guests toward Joshua Tree over the years, and I've watched people do it well and do it poorly. The poorly version usually involves showing up in July at noon with one water bottle and no reservation. The well version is more straightforward than you'd think.</p>

<img src="/blog/images/joshua-tree-national-park.jpg" alt="Joshua Tree National Park with clusters of Joshua trees and rounded granite boulders under a bright blue sky" width="1400" height="933" loading="lazy">

<h2>Two Entrances: Which One to Use</h2>

<p>The park has two main entry points from the Coachella Valley side, and the right one depends on where you're staying and what you want to see.</p>

<h3>South Entrance (Cottonwood) from Indio: 45 minutes</h3>

<p>If you're staying in Indio or the eastern valley, the Cottonwood South Entrance is your closest and least crowded option. Take the I-10 east to CA-195 north and you're at the entrance in about 45 minutes. This entrance puts you in the lower-elevation Colorado Desert section of the park, which has a different character than the boulder landscapes most people picture.</p>

<p>Cottonwood Spring Oasis near the entrance is worth a stop: a natural water source with tall fan palms and good birding. The Cottonwood Visitor Center here is small but useful. From Cottonwood, you can drive north through the park, stopping at the Cholla Cactus Garden along the way. The cholla cactus garden looks unremarkable in photos and stops you cold in person. Dense columns of jumping cholla, glowing when backlit, in a flat pan ringed by mountains. Give it 20 minutes.</p>

<h3>North Entrance (Twentynine Palms) or West Entrance (Yucca Valley) from Palm Springs: 1 hour 15 minutes</h3>

<p>From Palm Springs, take the I-10 east to CA-62 north. This takes you to the town of Yucca Valley and the West Entrance, or continue to Twentynine Palms and the North Entrance. Plan about 1 hour 15 minutes from Palm Springs to the West Entrance.</p>

<p>Most people who picture Joshua Tree picture this part of the park: the large granite boulder formations, the classic Joshua tree-covered landscape, the bouldering areas. Skull Rock is an easy roadside stop with a short loop trail. Hidden Valley is a short trail inside a natural enclosure of boulders that's genuinely impressive. Neither requires technical hiking ability. Keys View, near the north part of the park, gives you an elevated overlook of the full Coachella Valley below. On a clear day you can see the Salton Sea.</p>

<h2>Entry Fee</h2>

<p>The park entrance fee is $35 per vehicle, valid for 7 days. If you have an America the Beautiful annual pass, it covers Joshua Tree and is worth buying if you plan to visit multiple national parks in a year. The pass is $80 and pays for itself in three visits. Buy at the entrance or in advance at <a href="https://store.usgs.gov/america-the-beautiful" target="_blank" rel="noopener">store.usgs.gov</a>.</p>

<h2>Do You Need a Reservation?</h2>

<p>During peak periods, yes. As of 2026, Joshua Tree requires timed entry reservations on busy spring weekends. Coachella and Stagecoach festival weekends in April are high-demand periods when the entire valley fills up and the park sees spillover visitors. Check the <a href="https://www.nps.gov/jotr/planyourvisit/index.htm" target="_blank" rel="noopener">official Joshua Tree NPS page</a> well before you go and book ahead if your visit falls on a spring weekend.</p>

<p>Rangers turn people away at the entrance when reservations are required and you haven't booked. It happens to guests who assume they'll just show up. Weekday visits outside of peak season are generally walk-up friendly. The difference in crowd levels between a Tuesday and a Saturday in April is significant enough to change the trip.</p>

<p>Day trips don't require any special overnight permits. Overnight camping requires a separate reservation system through Recreation.gov.</p>

<h2>Best Time to Visit</h2>

<p>October through April is the sweet spot. Spring (February through April) brings wildflowers in good years and comfortable hiking temperatures in the 60s to low 70s. Fall is quieter, the light is extraordinary, and you avoid the spring crowds. Both seasons reward early starts.</p>

<p>Summer visits (June through September) are possible but require real planning. The higher elevations in the north part of the park are cooler than the valley floor, but midday is still brutal at any elevation. If you go in summer, arrive at first light and leave by noon. Bring more water than feels necessary.</p>

<p>Winter is underrated. The park is less crowded, temperatures are cool but comfortable for hiking, and the light in January and February is very good. If you're staying in the valley in winter, a Joshua Tree day trip is easy to pull off on a weekday without competing for parking.</p>

<h2>What to Bring</h2>

<p>This is where people underestimate the park. It looks accessible from the road and most of it is, but the desert is unforgiving for anyone who's underprepared.</p>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Water:</strong> A minimum of 1 gallon per person per day. More in summer or if you're hiking seriously. There is no potable water available inside most of the park. Fill up before entering.</li>
  <li><strong>Food and snacks:</strong> No food services inside the park. Bring more than you think you need.</li>
  <li><strong>Sun protection:</strong> Hat, sunscreen, and a light layer. The sun at 5,000 feet elevation hits differently than the valley floor.</li>
  <li><strong>Layers for evening:</strong> If you're planning to stay for sunset, temperatures drop fast once the sun goes down, even in spring.</li>
  <li><strong>Shoes:</strong> You'll be on rocky terrain on even the shortest trails. Sneakers are fine. Flip flops are not.</li>
  <li><strong>Offline maps:</strong> Cell service is limited inside the park. Download your route on Google Maps or AllTrails before you enter.</li>
</ul>

<h2>A Full Day Itinerary from Indio</h2>

<p>You can't see the whole park in one day. Here's a shape that works from Indio:</p>

<p><strong>7:30am:</strong> Leave Indio. The park is better in the morning. Lower temperatures, better light, fewer cars.</p>

<p><strong>8:15am:</strong> Cottonwood South Entrance. Stop at Cottonwood Spring Oasis (30 minutes). Pick up a park map at the visitor center.</p>

<p><strong>9:00am:</strong> Drive north to Cholla Cactus Garden. Walk the loop slowly (20 to 30 minutes). It glows when backlit.</p>

<p><strong>10:00am:</strong> Skull Rock Nature Trail (1.7 miles, easy loop) and Hidden Valley Trail (1 mile) if you want the boulder landscape.</p>

<p><strong>12:00pm:</strong> Keys View for the valley overlook. Five-minute walk from the parking lot. On a clear day you can see the Salton Sea.</p>

<p><strong>1:00pm:</strong> Head back. Be out before midday heat peaks in spring and summer. On the way, stop at Shields Date Garden in Indio (<a href="https://www.shields1924.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">shields1924.com</a>) for a date shake.</p>
