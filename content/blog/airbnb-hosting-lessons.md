<p>When I started hosting at <a href="/cozy-cactus/">The Cozy Cactus</a>, I thought the secret was somewhere obvious. Better pool. Nicer towels. A welcome bottle of wine on the counter. The kind of thing you'd expect to read in a "how to become a Superhost" listicle.</p>

<p>Four years and 191 reviews later, I can tell you almost none of that is what guests actually write about. Here's what is.</p>

<h2>The First 1-Star Review Was About a Garlic Press</h2>

<p>Not the pool. Not the bedroom. Not the location. A garlic press that was too small for the guest's needs.</p>

<p>That review stung, partly because it felt petty and partly because it was a fair complaint. We advertised a fully equipped kitchen. The kitchen technically had everything. It just wasn't stocked at the level guests expected. We revised the kitchen setup after that: full-size garlic press, proper chef's knife, a sheet pan that fits a whole chicken. We check every piece of equipment before each guest now.</p>

<p>The lesson wasn't about garlic presses specifically. It was that guests hold you to the standard your listing implies. If you say "fully stocked kitchen," you need to mean it at the level a capable home cook would mean it.</p>

<img src="/blog/images/cozy-cactus-family-kitchen.jpg" alt="Well-stocked family kitchen at The Cozy Cactus vacation rental in Indio California with labeled storage and warm natural light" width="1400" height="933" loading="lazy" decoding="async">
<p class="image-caption">The kitchen after the garlic press incident. Everything in here gets checked before every turnover.</p>

<h2>Response Speed Matters More Than Response Quality</h2>

<p>Responding in 5 minutes at 11pm earns you a 5-star review even when something goes wrong. Responding in 3 hours when everything is fine can lose a guest before the stay even starts.</p>

<p>This isn't about being available every second. It's about the psychology of uncertainty. When a guest sends a message, they're vulnerable. They're either about to book, just arrived somewhere new, or something is wrong. The faster you close that gap, the safer they feel. The safer they feel, the more generous they are in how they interpret everything else.</p>

<p>We use automated check-in messages for the routine stuff. But I stay personally available for anything that falls outside the script. The moments that matter most are the ones no automation anticipates.</p>

<h2>Stock Two of Everything, Then Add One More</h2>

<p>This sounds excessive until you run out of toilet paper at midnight and get a 3-star review about it.</p>

<p>We keep a spare roll behind the toilet in each bathroom, not just in the hall closet. We keep extra coffee filters inside the coffee drawer itself, not in a cabinet somewhere. Every bedside table has a phone charger cable. The pool towel rack has extras folded underneath.</p>

<p>The total cost of running this system across the whole house is maybe $20-30 per restocking. The review impact is real. Guests notice when things are just there when they need them. They notice even more when things aren't.</p>

<img src="/blog/images/cozy-cactus-labeled-drawers.jpg" alt="Organized and labeled kitchen drawers at The Cozy Cactus vacation rental in Indio California" width="1400" height="933" loading="lazy" decoding="async">
<p class="image-caption">Labeled drawers and stocked consumables. Small friction, eliminated before guests arrive.</p>

<h2>Temperature Control Is Binary</h2>

<p>Guests either love the default thermostat setting or they hate it. There is no middle ground.</p>

<p>For two months, we had a stretch where three different reviews mentioned the house being too hot and two mentioned it being too cold. Same thermostat setting each time. We eventually landed on 72 degrees as the starting point, with clear written instructions that it's easy to adjust. The reviews about temperature stopped almost immediately.</p>

<p>The instructions matter as much as the setting. Guests who know they're in control don't complain even if the default isn't perfect for them. Guests who aren't sure how the thermostat works blame the host when they're uncomfortable.</p>

<h2>The Putting Green Was the Best ROI We Ever Made</h2>

<p>We installed a small artificial putting green in the Cozy Cactus backyard for around $1,200. It gets mentioned in over 30 percent of reviews. That's not an exaggeration.</p>

<p>Guests don't write about things they expected to find. They write about things that surprised them. A putting green at a vacation rental is unexpected. It photographs well. It gives people something to do on a slow afternoon that doesn't require leaving the property. It becomes a shared story that the group talks about for the rest of the trip.</p>

<p>No amenity I've added since has moved the review needle the same way. If you're looking for one thing to add to a desert property that generates organic mentions in reviews, this is it.</p>

<h2>Photos Tell the Truth Eventually</h2>

<p>Your listing photo shows the living room styled perfectly. Your cleaning crew leaves the couch cushions slightly crooked. The first guest who notices that gap between the photo and reality will write about it.</p>

<p>We started photographing what guests actually find on arrival, not a staged version. The furniture is the same. The art is the same. But nothing is fluffed, nothing is arranged at its most photogenic, nothing looks like it's pretending. Guests who show up and find the house looking exactly like the photos have one less thing to feel deceived about.</p>

<p>Expectation gaps are the root of most complaints that aren't about actual problems. Closing them is free.</p>

<h2>Respond to Every Bad Review Within 24 Hours</h2>

<p>We had a 3-star review a couple years ago that was genuinely unfair. The guest complained about noise from a neighboring construction project that started the week they checked in, something we had no way to predict or control.</p>

<p>Our response took 48 hours and read defensive. That was a mistake. Future guests read host responses to bad reviews and use them to answer one question: is this host someone I can trust if something goes wrong? A calm, specific, accountable response says yes. A defensive or delayed response says the opposite.</p>

<p>The right move is always the same: respond within 24 hours, acknowledge what was valid, explain what you've done or what changed, keep it short. Don't argue. Don't say "as per our listing." Move on.</p>

<h2>Clean Is the Only Non-Negotiable</h2>

<p>Everything else in this post is optional. Cleanliness is not.</p>

<p>You don't get credit for a clean property. Guests expect it the way they expect the door to lock. But one cleaning miss, one hair on the soap dish, one sticky counter, and it colors everything. A guest who has a 9-out-of-10 stay will still lead their review with the thing that was visibly wrong if it was something they associate with hygiene.</p>

<p>We pay our cleaners above local market rate. We have a checklist that gets revised when a guest mentions something. It's not glamorous, but it's the foundation that everything else in this list is built on. Cut corners on cleaning and none of the other investments matter.</p>

<h2>What We Focus On Now</h2>

<p>We now run four properties across the Coachella Valley. The average is 4.9 stars. It wasn't always that way.</p>

<p>What I know now that I didn't know then: the review scores are mostly a measure of how well you anticipated what guests needed before they asked. Every point on this list is a version of that. Stock the consumables before they run out. Answer before they wonder if you're paying attention. Set the temperature to something reasonable and explain how to change it. Show them the putting green they didn't know they wanted.</p>

<p>Guests don't write about your couch. They write about how the stay made them feel. Most of the time, that comes down to whether someone thought about them before they arrived.</p>

<h2>FAQ: Vacation Rental Hosting</h2>

<h3>What vacation rental hosting tips actually move the needle on Airbnb reviews?</h3>
<p>Response time matters more than most hosts realize. Guests who hear back fast, even at odd hours, feel taken care of, and that shows in reviews. Beyond that: small friction-removing details like stocked consumables, pre-set temperatures with clear instructions, and unexpected amenities get called out in reviews far more often than expensive furniture does.</p>

<h3>How do you respond to a bad Airbnb review?</h3>
<p>Respond within 24 hours. Acknowledge what was valid, explain what you've fixed or changed, keep it warm and brief. Don't argue. Future guests read host responses to decide whether you're someone they can trust, and a calm, accountable reply builds more credibility than ignoring the complaint or pushing back on it.</p>

<h3>What unexpected amenities generate the most Airbnb reviews?</h3>
<p>In our experience: things guests don't expect to find but immediately use. A backyard putting green, a full-size kitchen stocked like someone actually cooks there, pre-assembled baby gear for families. Amenities that solve a specific problem or create a specific shared experience get mentioned in reviews at much higher rates than standard items like nice furniture or fast wifi.</p>

<h3>Is it worth paying cleaners above market rate for a vacation rental?</h3>
<p>Yes, without question. Cleanliness is the only factor that you get zero credit for when it's right and lose everything over when it's wrong. Professional cleaners with a detailed checklist and consistent pay produce consistent results. It's the foundation that every other hosting investment is built on.</p>
